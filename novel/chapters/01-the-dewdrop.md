# Chapter One — The Dewdrop

**Status:** drafted — pending Steven's approval  
**Time:** around age three; exact date not yet established  
**Place:** Everett, Washington  
**Primary source:** author testimony, preserved in the private autobiography source archive

My first memory begins when I was three years old and being moved into my second stepfather's home in Everett, Washington.

I was still wearing diapers when I was shown the room that would be mine on the third floor of the three-story house. I remember the house as a complete arrangement of levels, boundaries, and entrances.

A fence enclosed the yard. One set of outdoor steps descended toward it from above. A walkway crossed in front of those steps and led toward another row of stairs that climbed to the house.

At the top was a porch supported by two pillars. The first entrance was a thick wooden door with a glass window near the top. Inside was a coatroom where shoes, coats, and sports equipment were kept. Beyond it was another door, a sliding door with a looking window.

After we moved in, I sat at the top of the outdoor steps with my younger sister. We were both still in diapers. I could already speak. I looked at her and told her that I loved her.

Then I walked down the steps and onto the walkway leading toward the next row of stairs. Halfway between them, I turned to my left.

That was when I noticed a single blade of grass with a dewdrop resting at its tip.

I walked to the edge of the walkway and examined it. Beginning at the base, I drew my fingers toward the tip. The left side felt textured, sticky, and spiked. The right side felt completely smooth.

When I reached the end, I caught the dewdrop between my index finger and thumb. The water broke across my skin and was absorbed there. I felt what I can only describe as its full totality.

Then my attention widened.

I noticed the other blades of grass surrounding it. They could all be called blades of grass, but each one was also an individual object with its own characteristics. The name did not make them identical. The category did not erase the individual.

I became aware of the grass across the yard. I looked behind me at the blades extending through it. Then I turned back toward where my sister was sitting.

Near my original position, I picked a yellow daisy. It was one of the flowers we called wishing flowers.

I carried it back to my sister. I told her again that I loved her, and she made a wish.

This is my earliest complete memory.

It begins with being moved into a new house, but the move is not the part that remained most clearly. What remained was the structure of the house, the steps, the walkway, the difference between the two sides of one blade of grass, the dewdrop breaking across my fingers, the individual blades throughout the yard, my sister at the top of the steps, and the flower I carried back to her.

My earliest remembered discovery was that no two things disappear into the name we give them.

My earliest remembered gift was a flower carried back to my sister.

That is where my life story begins: with attention, individuality, water, love, and a wish.
