Chapter One of hi, my name is steven owens

Chapter One

The Dewdrop

Everett, Washington — age three

First-person autobiographical testimony Approved July 16, 2026 Approx. 11-minute read

My first memory begins with being moved into my second stepfather’s home in Everett, Washington. I was three years old. I was still wearing diapers when I was shown the room that would be mine on the third floor of the house.

I do not remember that beginning as a vague impression. I remember the structure of the place. The home had three stories and a fenced-in yard. Steps led down toward the yard from the upper level. At the bottom of those steps was a walkway, like a sidewalk running in front of the homes. Farther along it, another row of stairs led upward to the house itself.

The front porch had two pillars. The first entrance was a thick wooden door. Near the top of it was a glass window that allowed us to look outside. Passing through that door brought us into a coatroom. It was the place where shoes, coats, and sports equipment could be put away. Beyond the coatroom was another door. It operated as a sliding door and also contained a window for looking through.

Those details matter because this is how the memory exists for me: not only as an event, but as an arrangement. The steps are connected to the walkway. The walkway is connected to the second set of stairs. The stairs are connected to the porch. The porch is connected to the pillars, the thick wooden door, the window, the coatroom, and the sliding door beyond it. The memory has architecture.

After we had moved in, I was sitting at the top of the outdoor steps with my first sister. She had been born on January 1, 1985. We were both still in diapers. I could speak by then, and I told her that I loved her.

I walked down the steps and onto the walkway leading toward the other row of stairs. Halfway between the two sets of steps, I turned to my left. That was when I noticed a single blade of grass with a dewdrop resting at its tip.

I moved to the edge of the walkway and leaned toward it. I touched the blade at its base and slowly moved my fingers toward the top. The two sides did not feel the same. The left side had a texture that felt sticky and spiky. The right side was completely smooth.

At the tip was the dewdrop.

I placed it between my index finger and my thumb. As my fingers reached the end of the blade, the drop came away and spread into my skin. I felt the water in what I can only describe as its full totality. It was not simply wetness. It was the complete presence of that small amount of water at the exact place where my fingers met it.

Then my attention widened.

I noticed the other blades of grass surrounding the one I had touched. I understood that they could all be called blades of grass while still being individual objects. The shared name did not make them identical. Each blade occupied its own position. Each had its own length, angle, texture, shape, and condition. Each one was itself.

I became aware of the blades throughout the yard at once. I began remembering them at once. I looked behind me toward the grass in the rest of the yard, then turned back toward where I had been facing.

Near me was a yellow flower, one of the flowers we called wishing flowers. I picked it and carried it back to my sister.

I told her again that I loved her.

She made a wish.

That is my first memory.

It begins with relocation: a three-year-old child being moved into my second stepfather’s house and introduced to a room on the third floor. But the central action of the memory is not the move itself. It is attention. I remember a house by its passages and boundaries. I remember my sister beside me. I remember telling her I loved her. I remember leaving the steps, noticing something small, examining it, understanding something through it, and returning with a gift.

Looking back now, I can see that the memory contains several patterns that continued throughout my life.

The memory has architecture

The first is spatial memory. I remember places as systems. A building is not only an address or an exterior. It is a sequence of entrances, surfaces, levels, objects, and possible movements. The Everett house is preserved through the route into it: the fence, the yard, the descending steps, the walkway, the ascending steps, the porch, the two pillars, the wooden door, the looking window, the coatroom, and the sliding door beyond it. The environment is not background. It is part of the event.

The second pattern is sensory examination. I did not merely see the blade of grass. I followed it from its base to its tip. I compared one side with the other. I noticed opposition within one object: rough and smooth, resistant and effortless. Then I encountered the water at the end of it. The memory is visual, tactile, spatial, and conceptual at the same time.

The category did not erase the individual

The third pattern is classification without erasure. I understood that a group name can be useful without exhausting the reality of the individual things inside the group. Every blade was grass, but no blade was reducible to the word grass. The category connected them; it did not cancel their differences.

That realization became important far beyond the yard.

People are also placed into categories. Families have roles. Schools assign levels. Employers assign positions. Institutions assign classifications, diagnoses, risks, capacities, and case descriptions. Public audiences assign identities. Categories can help people communicate, but they can also become destructive when the name of the group is treated as a complete account of the individual.

My earliest remembered idea was that the individual remains present inside the category.

I did not have those adult words at three years old. I was not standing beside the walkway constructing a theory of classification. I was looking at grass. The philosophical language belongs to the person writing now. The perception belongs to the child who touched the blade.

The distinction matters. An autobiography should not place an adult essay inside a child’s mouth. But it should also not pretend that a child’s perception was empty merely because the child did not yet possess formal terminology for it.

What I remember is the recognition itself: these were all blades of grass, and each blade was its own object with its own characteristics.

Memory and evidence

The fourth pattern is memory as immediate accumulation. After noticing the first blade, I became aware of the others and began remembering them. This is consistent with how I have described my autobiographical memory throughout my life. I do not experience the past only as a thin summary of what happened. Some scenes remain available in arrangements of detail: where an object stood, how a surface felt, which direction I turned, and what one thing was connected to next.

That does not mean every remembered detail is automatically an independently verified fact. Memory and documentation are different forms of evidence. The date can be bounded by my age. My sister’s birth date provides another chronological anchor. An address, photograph, property record, or family recollection might someday corroborate the physical setting.

None of those records, however, could replace the internal event. A property record could establish that a house existed. It could not establish what the dewdrop felt like between my fingers or what occurred to me when I saw the individual blades of grass.

The return

The fifth pattern is return.

I walked away from my sister, but I returned to her. I did not return empty-handed. I brought the wishing flower. The movement of the memory is outward and back: from relationship, into observation, then back into relationship. I discovered something alone and carried something to someone I loved.

I cannot now know what my sister wished for. The wish belongs to her, even inside my memory. What belongs to me is the act of giving her the flower and telling her that I loved her.

This is significant because the earliest sentence I remember saying is not a declaration about myself. It is an expression of love toward another person. The first remembered object I studied was alive. The first remembered difference I examined was contained within a single blade. The first remembered abstraction concerned individuality. The first remembered gift was a flower.

A life does not always begin in autobiography where a birth certificate begins. A birth certificate records entry into the world. Autobiographical life begins at the first point from which the person can look backward and say:

I was there.
I saw this.
I touched this.
I understood something.
I loved someone.

For me, that point is a yard in Everett.

It is the top of a set of steps where two children sat in diapers. It is a walkway between one row of steps and another. It is a left turn. It is a blade of grass whose two sides felt different. It is a dewdrop transferred from the tip of the blade to my fingers. It is the sudden awareness that a field of similar things is also a field of individuals.

It is a yellow wishing flower carried back to my sister.

I told her I loved her.

She made a wish.

Verification note

This chapter is first-person autobiographical testimony. The approximate age, Everett location, house structure, outdoor steps, dewdrop, wishing flower, and remembered words are author-confirmed. The exact date, street address, and independent corroborating records remain open research items. No dialogue, person, motive, or event has been added beyond Steven’s account.
